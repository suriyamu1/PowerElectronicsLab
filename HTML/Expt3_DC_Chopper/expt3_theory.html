    • What is a Chopper?

    • A chopper is a static device which converts fixed input voltage to variable output voltage.
    • A chopper can be thought as a DC equivalent of an AC transformer as it steps-down or steps-up DC voltage.
    • The switching device used for a chopper circuit can be power BJT, power MOSFET, GTO or force-commutated thyristors (commutation circuit is needed if thyristors are used).
    • There are 2 types of choppers, Step-down chopper and step-up chopper. (Step-down chopper is more common)

    Simple Step Down Chopper (with resistive load)

    • A chopper is a high speed on/off semiconductor switches.
    • It connects source to load and disconnects the load from source at a fast speed.
    • These semiconductor switching devices can be generally represented by a switch (sw) with an arrow as shown.
    • When the switch is on, current flows in the direction of arrow. When the switch is off, no current can flow.
    • In this manner, a chopped load voltage (Vo) as shown, is obtained from a constant DC supply of magnitude (Vs)

    Operating Principle of Chopper

    • The switch is being operated with a duty cycle D
    • For a constant frequency, the duty cycle is defined as the ratio of the switch on time to the sum of switch on and switch off times.
    Modes of Operation of SCR
    • The following are the conditions for an SCR to conduct,
      1. The SCR must be forward biased (VAK > 0.6V)
      2. The gate pulse should be fired

    • Based on the above conditions, we have three modes of operation
      1. Forward Blocking Mode: SCR is forward biased but gate pulse is not applied.
      2. Forward Conduction Mode: SCR is forward biased and gate pulse was applied.
      3. Reverse Blocking Mode: SCR is reverse biased.

    • SCR only conducts in Forward Conduction Mode.
    • SCR never conducts in Reverse blocking mode regardless of whether the gate pulse is applied or not.
    Commutation of SCR
    • The process of turning off the SCR is called commutation.
    • SCR cannot be turned OFF using the gate terminal.
    • To turn off the SCR, the anode current must be reduced below the holding current of the SCR or commutation voltage must be applied across the SCR.

    • There are two types of commutation
      1. Natural Commutation
      2. Forced Commutation

    Natural Commutation

    • The source of commutation voltage is the AC voltage source itself.
    • This method is also called as Source Commutation or AC Line Commutation.
    • As the current in the AC circuit goes through the natural zero, a reverse voltage is applied immediately across the SCR
    • For successful natural commutation, the turn OFF time must be less than the duration of half cycle of the supply.
    Forced Commutation
    • In case of DC circuits, there is no natural current zero to turn OFF the SCR.
    • In this method, forward current must be reduced below holding current or reverse voltage voltage must be applied across the SCR with an external commutation circuit.

    Single Phase Semi Converter

    • This circuit utilises two SCR and two diodes.
    • SCRs are controlled rectifers and diodes are uncontrolled rectifiers. That is why semi converter provides limited control over output voltage and is known as semi-converter.
    • Semi converters are single quadrant converters
    • When inductor in the load discharges, negative output voltage will occur. The diodes in the circuit provide flywheeling path for the voltage stored in the inductor. Hence negative output voltage is not possible.
    • Diodes and SCRs will not allow reverse current, so negative current is not possible in semi-converter circuit
    • Since semi converter can only provide positive voltage and positive current, it always operates in First Quadrant
